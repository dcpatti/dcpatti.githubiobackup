Intake Demographics

Understanding the Animal Population Mix

Some species are at greater risk of surrender or stray capture than others.

Dogs and cats make up the two largest population blocks in this facility. This is fairly predictable, given that these are the two most common household pet types.

Cats actually outnumber dogs, which can be surprising to some, as third party animal rescue/adoption efforts for dogs can have a higher profile in some areas. This can give the perception that there are more dogs in shelters, when really there are just more groups trying to rescue them.

Drilling Into Intake Reasons by Species

Exploring the animal types and the intake reasons together can expose some interesting trends.

  • Cats are significantly more likely to be born in this facility than any other animal species.
  • "Born in Shelter" is also the most common reason for a cat to be taken into this facility. Expanding "Trap-Neuter-Release" (TNR) programs may help reduce shelter populations.
  • Dogs are more likely to be surrendered due to moving than other animal types are. Efforts to help pet owners connect with pet-friendly housing may help reduce this rate,
  • Dogs are also more likely to be surrendered due to relationship splits and affordability.
  • Rodents and wildlife animals are most likely to be taken in due to injury.
  • Reptiles and exotic pets are most likely to be surrendered as "incompatible". New Owner awareness classes could help reduce surrender rates.
  • Baby and young animals are the largest population blocks across almost all intake categories.
  • When senior and mature animals are taken in, the most common reasons are Stray and Incompatible.
  • Aging pets bring unique challenges that could contribute to their surrender rates, including declining health, decreased energy, and regression in housetraining. More research into this subset of animals could help identify ways to reduce the surrender rates.
  • Many young and baby animals are taken in due to compatibility, allergy, affordability and housing ("unsuitable accomodations") reasons, which suggests inexperienced pet owners that obtained their pets without sufficient preparation.
  • Moving affects all animal age groups nearly equally.
